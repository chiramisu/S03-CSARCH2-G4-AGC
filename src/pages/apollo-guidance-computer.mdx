## Introduction

        The Apollo Guidance Computer (AGC) was developed by the MIT Instrumentation Laboratory between 1961 and 1972. The AGC was one of the first computers to 
        rely on integrated circuits, which allowed it to meet the strict size, weight, and power restrictions required for the Apollo missions while maintaining 
        the reliability needed for mission operations.

        This push for miniaturization and dependability set new standards for digital computing. The innovations born from the AGC did not stay on the Moon. 
        Many of the technologies and design principles pioneered by the AGC later influenced modern smartphones, aircraft systems, and medical devices.

## All About the Apollo Guidance Computer (AGC)
<div class="agc-text">

    {/* IMAGE: AGC */}
    <div class="agc-image">
        <img src={dsky_agc.src} alt="The Apollo Guidance Computer" />
        <p class="caption">Apollo Guidance Computer. <a href="https://apollojournals.org/afj/compessay.html">Source: Apollo Flight Journal</a></p>
    </div>

    Developed by the MIT Instrumentation Laboratory, the Apollo Guidance Computer was the first computer built with <TermTooltip term="integrated circuits" info="Multiple transistors and other components combined onto a single small chip. This is what let the AGC be small and light enough for a spacecraft, instead of filling a whole room like earlier computers." /> and used <TermTooltip term="core memory" info="A type of memory made from tiny magnetized rings called cores. The AGC used it as erasable memory for data that changed during flight, an early version of what RAM does today." /> as well as 
    read-only <TermTooltip term="magnetic rope memory" info="A read-only memory woven by hand: a wire threaded through or around a tiny magnetic core encoded each bit. Because it was physically woven, the software inside could not be changed once the rope was built." /> (1968 | Timeline of Computer History | Computer History Museum, n.d.). Compared to older computers, which took up the space of a whole room, 
    it was compact and could fit inside a rocket ship. NASA also wanted the astronauts to be able to compute in real-time while in the spacecraft, instead of relying on on-ground, 
    analog computers (communication and computation using signals), which were neither reliable nor fast enough for a mission to the Moon (Atkinson, 2025). 
    
    The AGC also introduced architectural features that were uncommon at the time. It used erasable core memory for changing mission data and magnetic rope memory for permanently 
    storing software. By separating mission data from mission software, the system became more reliable because critical programs could not be modified during the flight. The magnetic 
    rope memory stored a large amount of software in a small and compact space, which was important due to the rocket’s weight limitations. In addition, the <TermTooltip term="DSKY (Display and Keyboard)" info="The AGC's interface panel: a small numeric keypad and display. Astronauts typed two-digit Verb and Noun codes into it to run programs and check spacecraft data mid-flight." /> 
    allowed astronauts to communicate directly with the computer, making it easier to monitor the spacecraft and perform navigation tasks in real time (Mattioli, 2021). 
    
    Another important feature of the AGC was its interrupt-driven design and <TermTooltip term="priority scheduling" info="A way of letting urgent tasks jump the queue instead of waiting their turn. This is exactly what saved the Apollo 11 landing: when the computer got overloaded and threw 1202 alarms, it kept running critical guidance tasks and dropped the less important ones." /> system, which allowed the computer to handle multiple tasks at once and prioritize critical 
    navigation and guidance calculations over less important processes. This enabled the AGC to continue operating even when it became overloaded (Sotomayor, 2016).

    The AGC did not just solve NASA's problems related to the Moon mission. It also addressed limitations of earlier computers, as it was significantly smaller than the vacuum tube- and 
    transistor-based computers through the use of integrated circuits. The AGC helped with the adoption of integrated circuits in the computer industry, making the way for smaller, more 
    reliable, and more portable electronic devices that we see today.

</div>

### Key specifications
<div class="agc-panel agc-mono">
    <b>Processor Speed:</b> ~2.048 MHz clock frequency, executing about 85,000 instructions per second.    <br></br>
    <b>Memory Architecture:</b> 16-bit word length (15 bits data + 1 bit parity).    <br></br>
    <b>RAM:</b> 2,048 words (approx. 4 KB) of magnetic-core erasable memory. <br></br>
    <b>ROM:</b> 36,864 words (approx. 72 KB) of fixed core-rope read-only memory.     <br></br>
    <b>Physical Dimensions:</b> 24 in × 12.5 in × 6.5 in (approx. 1 cubic foot).     <br></br>
    <b>Weight:</b> 70 lbs (32 kg).    <br></br>
    <b>Power Consumption:</b> 55 Watts at 28V DC.    <br></br>
</div>
