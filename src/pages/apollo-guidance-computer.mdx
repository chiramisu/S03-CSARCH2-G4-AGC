## Introduction
<div class="agc-panel">
    <div class="agc-row">
        <div class="agc-text">

        The Apollo Guidance Computer (AGC) was developed by the MIT Instrumentation Laboratory between 1961 and 1972. The AGC was one of the first computers to 
        rely on integrated circuits, which allowed it to meet the strict size, weight, and power restrictions required for the Apollo missions while maintaining 
        the reliability needed for mission operations.
        <br> </br>

        This push for miniaturization and dependability set new standards for digital computing. The innovations born from the AGC did not stay on the Moon. 
        Many of the technologies and design principles pioneered by the AGC later influenced modern smartphones, aircraft systems, and medical devices.

## All About the Apollo Guidance Computer (AGC)
<div class="agc-text">

    {/* IMAGE: AGC */}
    <div class="agc-image">
        <img src={dsky_agc.src} alt="The Apollo Guidance Computer" />
        <p class="caption">Apollo Guidance Computer. <a href="https://apollojournals.org/afj/compessay.html">Source: Apollo Flight Journal</a></p>
    </div>

    Developed by the MIT Instrumentation Laboratory, the Apollo Guidance Computer was the first computer built with integrated circuits and used core memory as well as 
    read-only magnetic rope memory (1968 | Timeline of Computer History | Computer History Museum, n.d.). Compared to older computers, which took up the space of a whole room, 
    it was compact and could fit inside a rocket ship. NASA also wanted the astronauts to be able to compute in real-time while in the spacecraft, instead of relying on on-ground, 
    analog computers (communication and computation using signals), which were neither reliable nor fast enough for a mission to the Moon (Atkinson, 2025). 
    
    The AGC also introduced architectural features that were uncommon at the time. It used erasable core memory for changing mission data and magnetic rope memory for permanently 
    storing software. By separating mission data from mission software, the system became more reliable because critical programs could not be modified during the flight. The magnetic 
    rope memory stored a large amount of software in a small and compact space, which was important due to the rocket’s weight limitations. In addition, the DSKY (Display and Keyboard) 
    allowed astronauts to communicate directly with the computer, making it easier to monitor the spacecraft and perform navigation tasks in real time (Mattioli, 2021). 
    
    Another important feature of the AGC was its interrupt-driven design and priority scheduling system, which allowed the computer to handle multiple tasks at once and prioritize critical 
    navigation and guidance calculations over less important processes. This enabled the AGC to continue operating even when it became overloaded (Sotomayor, 2016).

    The AGC did not just solve NASA's problems related to the Moon mission. It also addressed limitations of earlier computers, as it was significantly smaller than the vacuum tube- and 
    transistor-based computers through the use of integrated circuits. The AGC helped with the adoption of integrated circuits in the computer industry, making the way for smaller, more 
    reliable, and more portable electronic devices that we see today.

</div>
